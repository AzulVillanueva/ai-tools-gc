Introduction

Artificial Intelligence (AI) has become a key ally in today's workplace. From automating repetitive tasks to enhancing creativity and boosting productivity, its responsible and strategic use can deliver significant competitive advantages.

However, its widespread adoption also brings new challenges: improper use of sensitive information, overreliance, inaccurate results, or unnecessary licensing costs. That's why having a clear guide is essential to help teams integrate these tools effectively, ethically, and securely.

This guide outlines a set of general best practices applicable to any professional setting, followed by category-specific recommendations. The goal is to maximize the value of AI in daily work, while minimizing risks and fostering a culture of responsible innovation.

General Recommendations for Professional Use of AI

To ensure AI tools are truly effective in the workplace, they must be used strategically and responsibly. By following these best practices, teams can optimize AI performance, safeguard information, and avoid pitfalls like overdependence or unnecessary expenses. Below are the key guidelines for proper use.

1. Choose and Use the Right Tool

  • Use specialized tools based on the task (e.g., GitHub Copilot for development, ChatGPT for writing, Midjourney for visual design).
  • Assess the maturity and reliability of the tool for the problem you're solving.
  • Leverage integrations with existing platforms like Microsoft 365 or Google Workspace.
  • Create reusable templates for frequent tasks such as emails, reports, or summaries.

2. Validate Outputs and Apply Human Judgment

  • Treat AI-generated results as drafts—not absolute truths.
  • Pay special attention when reviewing technical, legal, financial, or data analysis content.
  • Combine AI input with professional judgment when making decisions or sharing outcomes.
  • Use AI as a co-pilot, not a replacement for your skills.

3. Protect Information and Comply with Regulations

  • Do not input sensitive, confidential, or proprietary information into non-corporate tools.
  • Ensure tools comply with standards like GDPR, SOC 2, ISO 27001, or HIPAA.
  • Use enterprise-grade versions with security controls and administrative management.
  • Avoid linking personal accounts to work-related AI tools.

4. Cybersecurity

  • Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA).
  • Use secure password managers like Bitwarden.
  • Monitor access and activity using SIEM tools.
  • Train the team to recognize phishing, fraud, and impersonation attempts.
  • Conduct cybersecurity audits in collaboration with IT teams.

5. Efficient License and Cost Management

  • Centralize license management under corporate accounts.
  • Evaluate whether free versions meet your team's needs.
  • Opt for scalable team plans instead of individual licenses.
  • Audit usage quarterly to remove unused licenses.
  • Group tools by function to avoid redundancy.

6. Integration into the Work Environment

  • Embed AI into everyday workflows based on roles and tasks.
  • Connect tools using platforms like Zapier or Make.com.
  • Define usage standards by team or function.
  • Train teams using real business use cases.

7. Ongoing Training and Upskilling

  • Stay up to date on new tools and features.
  • Join webinars, read changelogs, and follow trusted sources.
  • Train teams on responsible, ethical, and efficient AI use.
  • Foster a culture of informed experimentation and knowledge sharing.

8. Ethical and Transparent Use

  • Disclose when AI is used to generate content, analysis, or automation.
  • Avoid using AI to manipulate, impersonate, or spread misinformation.
  • Promote transparency in processes that involve AI.

9. Continuous Measurement and Improvement

  • Evaluate the impact of AI use on productivity, quality, speed, or creativity.
  • Adjust tools or usage practices based on observed results.
  • Set clear metrics to measure the effectiveness of AI adoption across teams.

Category-Specific Recommendations for AI Use

In addition to general best practices, it's essential to consider the specific nature of each task when applying AI in daily work. Using AI to write documents is very different from using it to analyze data, assist in meetings, or generate code.

That's why the following section outlines best practices tailored to different use cases, helping teams adopt AI tools more effectively, safely, and ethically within their specific contexts.

Note-Taking and Meeting Management

AI-powered tools for transcribing, summarizing, and organizing meetings can significantly enhance efficiency. However, they also require special attention to privacy, accuracy, and participant experience. Below are some key recommendations:

Best Practices for Better Outcomes

  • Customize generated summaries: use prompts or templates to focus outputs on decisions, action items, or key points.
  • Supplement with personal notes: don't rely solely on the AI note-taker. Add context or important elements that the tool might overlook.
  • Review and edit before sharing: double-check names, decisions, and key concepts to ensure the summary accurately reflects the conversation.
  • Generate action follow-ups: use AI to turn meeting notes into tasks or reminders, and integrate them into your project management tools.

Best Practices to Avoid Risks

  • Request consent before recording or transcribing: inform all participants that an AI tool will be used to record, transcribe, or summarize the meeting. Never record without prior consent, especially when external guests or clients are involved. Set up privacy alerts before recording conversations.
  • Avoid adding virtual assistants by default: don't automatically include note-taking bots or AI assistants in every meeting. Evaluate on a case-by-case basis whether they truly add value. Avoid full dependency—complement AI with essential personal notes.
  • Respect organizational privacy policies: ensure the note-taking tool complies with internal guidelines or applicable regulations such as GDPR or other relevant local privacy laws in your region.
  • Limit access to AI-generated notes: share summaries or transcripts only with participants or those with a direct need-to-know role. Avoid storing sensitive conversations on platforms without encryption or administrative controls.

Data Analysis and Automation

AI applied to data analysis enables the detection of patterns, prediction of trends, and automation of processes that enhance decision-making. However, its use demands both technical and ethical responsibility to prevent errors, bias, or misinterpretation. Here are some recommended practices:

Best Practices for Better Outcomes

  • Explore data with AI assistance: use AI to speed up exploratory analysis, generate charts, identify trends, and create preliminary reports.
  • Promote data literacy: train teams to interpret results and understand the basics of the models being used.
  • Document rules, thresholds, and models: ensure traceability and clarity about how the AI is working—especially important in regulated environments.
  • Use AI for repetitive analysis tasks: automate recurring reports, data cleaning, or basic validations to free up time for more strategic analysis.

Best Practices to Avoid Risks

  • Always validate AI-generated outputs: don't blindly trust predictions, dashboards, or automated insights. Review the logic, context, and assumptions behind the results.
  • Know the source and quality of your data: ensure that data is clean, up to date, and accurately represents business reality. AI is only as reliable as the data it's given.
  • Avoid automating critical processes without oversight: sensitive processes—such as financial decisions, hiring, or customer segmentation—should include human review or additional validation.
  • Detect and mitigate bias: training models on historical data can reinforce past errors or discrimination. Assess whether algorithms are fair, transparent, and explainable.
  • Set up alerts and continuous monitoring: in automated processes, define control points to detect anomalies or deviations in real time.

Writing, Summarization and Translation

AI tools are especially helpful for drafting clear text, summarizing large volumes of information, or translating content quickly. However, it's essential to apply professional judgment to avoid errors, misunderstandings, or a loss of communication quality. Key recommendations include:

Best Practices for Better Outcomes

  • Clearly define the objective before writing: specify the type of text, target audience, tone, and purpose to improve the quality of AI-generated outputs.
  • Edit and adapt generated content: use AI as a starting point, but adjust the draft to match your context and your organization's voice.
  • Leverage smart assistance tools: take advantage of contextual spell checkers, templates, and writing suggestions to enhance style, grammar, and cohesion.
  • Optimize repetitive tasks: use AI to draft emails, summarize meeting minutes, write product descriptions, or prepare executive reports.
  • Maintain a human voice in communication: prioritize empathy, authenticity, and a personal tone—especially in sensitive or high-impact communications.

Best Practices to Avoid Risks

  • Review terminology and precision, especially in translations: in technical or legal contexts, human review is essential.
  • Avoid delegating critical content to AI: for contracts, statements, or policies, always involve a professional for oversight.
  • Do not input sensitive information into public tools: avoid using confidential content in platforms without privacy controls or encryption.
  • Avoid indiscriminate use of AI-generated content: do not copy and paste without review—always curate and validate before publishing or sharing.

Design, Creativity and Presentations

AI tools can enhance creativity, accelerate the production of visual materials, and facilitate the creation of impactful presentations. However, effective use requires a balance between automation and human judgment.

Best Practices for Better Outcomes

  • Clearly define the visual objective and audience: specify the style, tone, format, and intended use (e.g., social media, internal presentation, publication) to improve the quality of the generated proposals.
  • Iterate with specific prompts: visual and generative tools (such as Midjourney, DALL·E, or presentation tools like Tome and Canva Magic) perform better when commands are detailed and progressive.
  • Combine AI with design principles: apply basic principles like visual hierarchy, color balance, and readability. AI suggests, but the designer decides.
  • Personalize generated templates and assets: adjust AI-generated visual elements to maintain consistency with your organization's or project's visual identity.
  • Use AI for brainstorming: leverage generative tools to quickly explore creative variants, overcome creative blocks, facilitate inspiration, or expand on initial concepts.

Best Practices to Avoid Risks

  • Review usage rights for generated images: ensure that the generated content does not infringe on licenses or copyrights, especially if used in public or commercial materials.
  • Avoid over-reliance on generic templates: while useful as a starting point, templates can undermine authenticity or become repetitive across organizations.
  • Do not use generated images without validation: in sensitive contexts (e.g., institutional, educational, or informational content), visual representation must be accurate and ethical.
  • Ensure visual quality in key deliverables: check resolution, typography, and format compatibility before exporting or printing generated materials.
  • Maintain a coherent narrative: ensure that visual elements, text, and data align with each other and with the core message.

Search, Content Organization and Productivity

AI can be a powerful ally in optimizing time management, organizing information, and speeding up decision-making. From sorting emails and tasks to summarizing lengthy documents, these tools help improve focus and efficiency. To leverage their potential without compromising quality or control, it's essential to follow best practices.

Best Practices for Better Outcomes

  • Apply intelligent summaries for quick reading: use AI tools to summarize long emails, minutes, reports, or presentations.
  • Use AI to categorize emails, tasks, and documents: classify them according to context and priority to streamline workflow.
  • Set personalized criteria for more precise and relevant contextual searches.
  • Integrate AI with calendars, task managers, or CRM tools: automate activity prioritization to stay organized.
  • Leverage virtual assistants for reminders, task tracking, or meeting preparation.

Best Practices to Avoid Risks

  • Always review automated recommendations before accepting them: this includes suggested responses, rescheduling, or categorizations.
  • Don't delegate critical decisions (such as task assignments or major reschedules) without human validation.
  • Validate the access productivity tools have to sensitive calendars, emails, or documents.
  • Avoid uploading personal or confidential information into tools lacking robust security policies.
  • Monitor AI usage in time management to prevent fostering a culture of overproductivity or excessive surveillance.

Audio and Voice Editing

AI tools applied to audio can improve sound quality, accelerate podcast editing, and generate synthetic voices for multimedia content. However, their use also involves ethical and technical risks that need to be considered, especially when working with public or third-party content.

Best Practices for Better Outcomes

  • Use AI to remove background noise, enhance audio clarity, and edit recordings quickly and accurately.
  • Leverage synthetic voices to create narrations, prototypes, or preliminary versions of voiceovers.
  • Use AI to adjust speed, tone, or pitch of the voice according to the target audience.
  • Incorporate AI for automatic transcriptions to facilitate subtitling or documentation.
  • Verify lip-syncing if using AI-generated voice combined with video.

Best Practices to Avoid Risks

  • Clearly notify if AI-generated voice is used in public content or for commercial purposes.
  • Deepfakes: do not use synthetic voices to simulate identities or manipulate content. Avoid cloning real voices without the explicit consent of the individual involved.
  • Review the licenses and terms of use for the tools used, especially if the content is monetized.
  • Prevent leaks: protect both original and edited audio files in secure environments.

Workflow Automation

AI-driven automation allows for the orchestration of repetitive tasks and workflows across multiple applications, speeding up processes and reducing human errors. While these tools are highly effective, their implementation must be approached with care to avoid bottlenecks, silent failures, or maintenance issues.

Best Practices for Better Outcomes

  • Map the entire workflow before automating: understanding all steps and variations in the process helps design efficient and sustainable automations.
  • Document automated processes: recording inputs, outputs, conditions, and exceptions facilitates collaboration and long-term maintenance.
  • Set control points or manual validation in critical workflows: for sensitive decisions, incorporating human reviews prevents dangerous or incorrect automations.
  • Review implemented workflows periodically: tools, APIs, and needs change over time. Auditing and adjusting workflows ensures they remain relevant and efficient.

Best Practices to Avoid Risks

  • Avoid automating without understanding the process: lack of comprehension can lead to logical errors or loss of important data.
  • Explicitly manage errors and exceptions: include alerts or contingency paths for failures or incomplete data.
  • Control access to sensitive automations: restrict who can modify or activate critical workflows to protect security and operational continuity.
  • Avoid "automation for the sake of it": not everything needs to be automated. Evaluate whether the effort is worth the ROI and maintenance.

Specialized Tools

Industry-specific AI tools (such as those for healthcare, law, finance, or manufacturing) offer significant advantages as they are tailored to their respective domains. However, they also require more careful use due to their potential impact.

Best Practices for Better Outcomes

  • Evaluate the reputation, support, and track record of the tool: research use cases, references, and the adoption level within the industry.
  • Choose tools with customization options or domain-specific training: greater alignment with specific requirements improves results.
  • Leverage advanced features like auditing, logging, or user roles: these capabilities strengthen governance and traceability.
  • Consult domain experts: input from professionals in the field helps interpret results and configure the tool correctly.

Best Practices to Avoid Risks

  • Always validate AI-generated results: especially in technical, medical, or legal contexts where errors can have serious consequences.
  • Set limits, alerts, and usage controls: prevent excess, data leaks, or automatic decisions without review.
  • Comply with industry regulations: use tools with certifications like HIPAA, ISO 27001, or GDPR, as applicable.
  • Review terms of use and licenses: some providers may store data or use the results for training unless contractually restricted.

Virtual Assistants - Conversational AI

Conversational AI-powered virtual assistants like ChatGPT, Copilot, Gemini, and Claude have become versatile allies for answering questions, generating ideas, drafting content, coding, and automating daily tasks. Their ability to process natural language and adapt to different contexts makes them valuable in professional, educational, and creative environments. However, to get accurate and coherent responses, it's essential to interact with these tools in a strategic and thoughtful way.

Best Practices for Better Outcomes

  • Provide context in your prompts: when interacting with a virtual assistant, include enough background so it can generate more accurate and relevant answers. For example, mention the topic, target audience, or desired format.
  • Give clear and specific instructions: the more detailed and precise your input, the better the AI's response will be. If you're asking about a technical topic, include key terms and specify the structure you expect.
  • Encourage reasoning and detailed answers: when asking for explanations or solutions, request that the AI explain its reasoning or outline the steps it followed. This leads to more complete answers and helps identify logical gaps.
  • Adjust tone and style for your audience: if you're using conversational AI for customer service or internal communication, tailor the tone of the responses to fit the audience's expectations—whether formal, friendly, technical, etc.
  • Leverage its summarization capabilities: use virtual assistants to distill large volumes of information into clear, concise summaries that highlight key points.
  • Review responses before sharing: always double-check AI-generated responses before sharing them with others—especially in formal or sensitive contexts. While AI can offer relevant insights, it's crucial to validate and refine the output when needed.

Best Practices to Avoid Risks

  • Don't assume the AI fully understands context: despite their capabilities, virtual assistants may miss nuances or specific details. Provide comprehensive information and clarify as needed.
  • Avoid delegating critical decisions without oversight: these tools are great for brainstorming and suggestions, but important decisions—such as business strategy or legal matters—should always be reviewed by a human.
  • Don't rely entirely on AI for complex tasks: tools like ChatGPT, Copilot, Gemini, and Claude are powerful, but they're not infallible. Users must stay actively involved to ensure outputs are correct and actionable.
  • Protect privacy and sensitive data: when interacting with AI assistants, avoid sharing confidential, personal, or sensitive information. Always review the platform's privacy policies and terms of use to understand how data is handled.
  • Manage ambiguous or off-target answers: if the AI gives incomplete, inaccurate, or inappropriate responses, refine your prompt or provide additional context. Rephrasing often improves the quality of interaction.

Image and Video Generation

The generation of visual content with AI accelerates creative processes, enables the creation of prototypes, graphics, or even audiovisual material from text. Tools like Midjourney expand the possibilities, but it is crucial to use them responsibly.

Best Practices for Better Outcomes

  • Clearly define the prompt: The more specific the input text, the better the results and the lower the risk of generating inappropriate images.
  • Iterate and adjust: Test multiple versions of the prompt and review the results before publishing or using them.
  • Use tools like Pika or Runway for animations: Facilitate the creation of dynamic or interactive content from static images.
  • Include human review before publishing: Verify the quality, visual consistency, and contextual appropriateness of the generated content.

Best Practices to Avoid Risks

  • Check the usage rights of generated content: Not all results are free for commercial use and may include protected styles.
  • Avoid generating images of real people without consent: This includes celebrities, colleagues, or public figures, to prevent legal and ethical issues.
  • Do not use generated content for manipulation or misinformation: Such as deepfakes, misleading images, or political uses without validation.
  • Be cautious with sensitive or violent content: Some tools may generate inappropriate results if prompts are not carefully controlled.

Coding and Assisted Development

AI applied to software development accelerates tasks like code generation, refactoring, documentation, and testing, enhancing productivity. Tools like GitHub Copilot, Cursor, and Amazon CodeWhisperer allow AI to be integrated into the development cycle.

Best Practices for Better Outcomes

  • Use AI to accelerate repetitive tasks: Such as creating standard functions, unit tests, or adding comments to code.
  • Request explanations alongside the suggested code: Understanding why a certain solution is proposed helps to learn and detect potential errors.
  • Complement with automated testing tools: Ensure that the suggested code meets functional and quality requirements.
  • Document what is incorporated from AI: Keep clarity about which fragments were generated and reviewed.

Best Practices to Avoid Risks

  • Always review the code suggested by AI: It may contain logical errors, security issues, or inefficient practices.
  • Do not share sensitive business logic or credentials: Even if the environment is secure, avoid exposing strategic information.
  • Avoid blind dependency on the tool: AI does not replace technical judgment or peer reviews.
  • Review usage licenses and intellectual property of the generated code: Some platforms may impose restrictions or claims regarding its use.
